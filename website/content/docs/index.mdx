Welcome to the **nestjs-notifications** documentation.

`@dudousxd/nestjs-notifications-core` brings Laravel's notification ergonomics to NestJS. You write a
notification once — a plain class that lists its channels and shapes a payload for each — and
send it with a single call. It fans out to mail, the database, a websocket, Slack, or anything
you plug in, synchronously or queued, with one call site that never changes.

Head to the [Getting Started](/docs/getting-started) guide to send your first notification in
under five minutes, or use the sidebar to explore the concepts, channels, and dispatchers.

## What you get

- **One notification, many channels.** Define `via()` and a `to<Channel>()` method per channel.
  Mail, database, broadcast and Slack ship today; each is an opt-in package.
- **Sync or queued, without changing the call site.** Deliver inline by default, or set
  `shouldQueue` and route through an async dispatcher — in-process events, Redis, or the BullMQ
  you already run.
- **A decoupled core.** The core knows only interfaces. Channels are discovered from the Nest
  container; dispatchers are swapped with one option. Nothing is hard-wired.
- **Type-safe payloads.** Each channel exports an interface your notification implements, so
  `toMail`, `toDatabase` and `toSlack` are checked at compile time.
- **On-demand routing.** No entity to notify? Send straight to an address with
  `notifications.route('mail', 'ops@x.com').notify(...)`.
- **Observable.** Lifecycle events for every send, plus a first-class
  [nestjs-telescope](/docs/recipes/telescope) watcher that records each delivery.
- **Built to test.** A `NotificationFake` swaps the real service in tests, with Laravel-style
  assertions and no real SMTP, sockets, or queues.

## Why

Sending a notification is rarely "send an email." It's an email *and* a database row for the
in-app feed *and* a realtime push *and*, when something is on fire, a Slack message — and half
of those should happen on a queue, not on the request. Wire that by hand and the logic for one
event ends up smeared across a mailer, a repository, a gateway, and a queue processor.

nestjs-notifications collapses that into a single class. The recipient decides *where* it goes
(`routeNotificationFor`), the notification decides *what* each channel sends (`to<Channel>()`),
and the library handles the fan-out, the failures, and — when you ask — the queue.

If you've used Laravel notifications, you already know this API. If you haven't,
[Getting Started](/docs/getting-started) is five minutes.
